About

The Question

The question I find most valuable is not "how do we implement AI"; it is where would the right AI solution create genuine value? In power systems that might mean forecasting demand accurately enough to change how a utility plans its day, detecting a fault before it cascades, or surfacing an insight from operational data that was simply not visible before. Over five years across energy, utilities, and applied research, I have built my practice around that distinction: finding the problems where AI can make a real difference, and then doing the rigorous work of realising them.

The Method

My formal training in electrical power engineering and artificial intelligence gives me the depth to move fluently across a wide methodological toolkit — from classical machine learning and time-series forecasting to deep learning, metaheuristics, and reinforcement learning. This demands more than technical proficiency; it requires a clear understanding of the underlying power system, sound judgement on whether AI is truly the right answer, and the foresight to choose an approach that will genuinely deliver in the field.

The Delivery

The distance between a technically sound model and an operationally trusted one is almost never algorithmic. It is a question of how clearly the solution is communicated, how naturally it integrates into existing grid and utility workflows, and how confidently the engineers who depend on it learn to use it. Closing that distance — turning a strong model into something an organisation actually relies on — is where the real work lies.
